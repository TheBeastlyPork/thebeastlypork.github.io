## Heading

Before going any further, there's some loose points we'll need to agree to for any of this to make sense:
1. There are actually moral standards, and we should care about understanding them.
2. Whatever sense of morality most people have is more or less the right idea.
3. Artificial Intelligence operates by expanded moral standards than existing tools.
   
  (1) and (2) are just so we don't spend all day arguing about metaethics, but I believe (3) is also critical to move forward in this conversation. I have personally never heard anyone argue against (3) (nor have I heard anyone deny (1) or (2)!) yet there's a distinct societal conversation around tools that's complicated enough as it is without autonomy; is a tool (including weapons?) morally neutral, who's at fault for tool misuse, responsibilities to inform users, what's the right way to use a tool, etc. I think it's fair to call this ethics-of-tools "tech ethics." I posit that the distinction between tech ethics and robotics ethics is that there's no autonomy within the tool. Your drill is not making decisions about how fast it wants to spin for you! Plenty of robotics and AI ethics certainly is covered by tech ethics, notably in situations where "dumber" robots follow very simple instructions, but I believe we should also note we're in new territory. Robotics force us to answer new and exciting questions about ethics that tech ethics doesn't quite reach.
